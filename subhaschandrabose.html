Subhas Chandra Bose

About Subhas Chandra Bose

Subhas Chandra Bose (1897–1945) was a prominent Indian nationalist leader who fought for India’s independence with unmatched courage and vision. He believed that freedom could not be achieved by non-violence alone and took bold steps toward armed struggle.

He led the **Indian National Army (INA)** and coined the slogan **“Give me blood, and I will give you freedom.”** His efforts to seek international support and his alliance with Axis powers during World War II marked a radical phase in the independence movement.

Though his death remains a mystery, Bose is remembered as **“Netaji”** — a title of honor for his fearless leadership and sacrifice for India’s independence.

Story

A Leader is Born

In the bustling town of Cuttack in Odisha, on **23 January 1897**, a child was born to Janakinath Bose and Prabhavati Devi. (A Leader is Born) Raised in a large, affectionate Bengali family, young Subhash absorbed the values of self-respect, duty, and love for the motherland. His father was a respected lawyer, and his mother’s gentle strength shaped his sense of compassion and discipline.

A Brilliant Student with a Patriotic Heart

As he grew, Subhash showed a sharp mind and a quiet determination. (A Brilliant Student with a Patriotic Heart) At Ravenshaw Collegiate School and later at Presidency College in Calcutta, he excelled in studies but never accepted injustice. An incident of British professor-student conflict at Presidency disturbed him deeply, and he began to see education as more than grades—it was a path to serve India.

Choosing the Nation Over a Secure Career

After graduating, he traveled to England to prepare for the **Indian Civil Service (ICS) examination** and succeeded with high merit. (Choosing the Nation Over a Secure Career) Yet his conscience tugged at him. Serving a colonial government did not match his ideals. He **resigned** before taking up the post—an uncommon and courageous step—declaring that his real duty was to fight for India’s freedom.

Learning Leadership

Returning to India in the early 1920s, Subhash plunged into the freedom movement under the influence of Swami Vivekananda’s teachings and the call of Mahatma Gandhi’s mass politics. (Learning Leadership) He organized students, wrote passionately for newspapers, and earned a reputation for tireless work, discipline, and a fearless voice against oppression.

Rising to National Leadership

His abilities soon brought him to the front ranks of the Indian National Congress. (Rising to National Leadership) Elected Mayor of Calcutta and later Congress President at Haripura in 1938 and again in 1939, Bose argued for complete independence and rapid industrialization. Differences with senior leaders on the pace and method of struggle led him to step down and form the **Forward Bloc**, a platform to unite all anti-colonial forces.

The Great Escape

British authorities repeatedly imprisoned him, hoping to silence his influence. (The Great Escape) In **1941**, while under house arrest in Calcutta, he slipped away in disguise, traveled north by train and road through Peshawar and Kabul, and reached Europe. His journey was daring and dangerous, driven by a single aim—to find international allies to free India.

Azad Hind in Europe

In Germany, Bose set up the Free India Centre and raised the **Indian Legion** from prisoners of war. (Azad Hind in Europe) He broadcast stirring messages to Indians, calling for unity beyond region, religion, or caste. Yet, as the tides of the war shifted, he looked eastward to where a larger Indian force could be organized.

To the East for Freedom

Undaunted by risk, Bose crossed oceans during wartime, transferring between submarines in a remarkable voyage to reach East Asia. (To the East for Freedom) In 1943 he arrived in Japanese-held territory, where thousands of Indian soldiers and civilians awaited a leader who matched their hope with action.

The Birth of Azad Hind

Taking command of the **Indian National Army (INA)**, he reorganized it with strict discipline and a clear goal—march to Delhi. (The Birth of Azad Hind) On **21 October 1943**, he proclaimed the Provisional Government of Azad Hind in Singapore, adopted **‘Jai Hind’** as a unifying salutation, rallied the nation with ‘Give me blood, and I will give you freedom,’ and set up the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment**, a pioneering all-women combat unit.

Delhi Chalo

In 1944, INA units fought alongside Japanese forces toward India’s eastern frontiers. (Delhi Chalo) The INA flag was raised at **Moirang in Manipur**, symbolizing the nearness of the dream. But the fierce battles of Imphal and Kohima, monsoon, disease, and supply shortages turned the tide. The INA retreated with courage intact but resources spent.

A Spark That Lit Millions

Though battlefield victory eluded them, the INA’s story shook British confidence. (A Spark That Lit Millions) The Red Fort trials of INA officers stirred unprecedented public sympathy across India—Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, and others rallied together. The idea that Indian soldiers had fought under an Indian banner electrified the country and weakened colonial authority from within.

The Unfinished Journey

In August 1945, as the war neared its end, news spread that Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose had died in an **air crash in Taiwan on 18 August**. (The Unfinished Journey) Questions about the circumstances remained, and debates would continue for decades. Yet, beyond the mystery stood a clear truth—his life had become a symbol of fearless action for freedom.

Legacy of a Hero

Today, Subhash Chandra Bose’s name evokes courage, sacrifice, and purposeful leadership. (Legacy of a Hero) He taught that freedom demands unity, discipline, and readiness to give one’s all. His call—**‘Jai Hind’**—still rings as a national salute, reminding every generation that love for the motherland is shown not only in words but in determined deeds.

Famous Quotes

  • “Give me blood, and I shall give you freedom!”
  • “It is blood alone that can pay the price of freedom. Give me blood and I will give you freedom.”
  • “Freedom is not given, it is taken.”
